⚠ Closed — don't plan around these
Biscuit Basin is closed. A hydrothermal explosion on July 23, 2024 destroyed the boardwalk and parking lot. Officials fenced it off "until further notice" and it has stayed closed since — assume it's still closed for our trip.

Because the closure covers the whole basin and its parking lot, these are all off the table too — they're either features on the Biscuit Basin boardwalk or trails that start from its lot:

• Sapphire Pool — the famous deep-blue pool (the explosion was centered right here)
• Jewel Geyser — the small geyser that erupts every 7–10 minutes
• Black Diamond Pool, Black Opal Pool, Mustard Spring, Wall Pool
• Mystic Falls — the 70-ft falls hike; its trailhead sits inside the closed lot

What we do instead: Black Sand Basin and Fountain Paint Pot (both on today's route) cover very similar ground. Worth a quick check of nps.gov before we leave in case it reopens.
⚠ Firehole Lake Drive — the time trap. Skip unless the geyser is predicted
The draw: Great Fountain Geyser shoots water 100–200 ft in the air and is genuinely spectacular.

The problem: it erupts only once every 9–15 hours, and even the official prediction carries a two-hour window. Show up at the wrong moment and you're looking at a still pool of water.

The trap: people decide to "wait it out" — and burn 90 minutes of prime park time staring at nothing. The rest of the loop (White Dome Geyser, Firehole Lake) is visually underwhelming, so there's nothing to fall back on. Meanwhile we're doing Fountain Paint Pot and Grand Prismatic the same day — dense, high-action boardwalks that deliver every single time.

The rule: at the Old Faithful visitor center in the morning, check the Great Fountain prediction. If it doesn't line up with a ~2:30pm arrival, drive straight past and go home early. Do not wait for it. There is no version of this where waiting is the right call.

⚠ Boiling River is closed — don't plan on it
If anyone in the group has heard about Boiling River — the famous soaking spot near Mammoth where a hot spring poured into the cold Gardner River — it no longer exists as a swimming area.

The June 2022 floods reshaped the riverbed entirely. The walkway was swept away, the banks eroded, the soaking pools are gone, and the geothermal vents shifted so the scalding water no longer mixes with the cold river. The access road was destroyed too — the old site now sits about 3 miles from any road, with no safe or legal way in. As of 2026 there is no plan to reopen it.

The only legal swim in Yellowstone is the Firehole Swimming Area — that's on Day 3 (Firehole Canyon Drive). It's river water, so it's cool rather than warm, but it's the real thing.

Yellowstone
🧭 Where in the park: The east-central park, where the Upper and Lower Loops meet — the Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone, a deep river gorge with two big waterfalls, plus open wildlife flats just to the south.
🗺️ Open Day 6 route in Google Maps →
East side of the figure-8. West → Norris → Canyon, then work both rims of the canyon plus Hayden Valley just south. The most viewpoints of any day — pace it out.
First, how the canyon works — so the viewpoints make sense
"Yellowstone Falls" is really two waterfalls on the Yellowstone River: the Upper Falls (109 ft) and, just downstream, the big famous Lower Falls (308 ft — more than twice the height of Niagara). Both sit at the head of the Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone, a 1,000-ft-deep gorge of orange, yellow, and pink rock.

You see them from about ten named overlooks split across two rims, each its own road off the Grand Loop:
• North Rim Drive (one-way loop) — Brink of the Lower Falls, Lookout Point, Grand View, Inspiration Point
• South Rim Drive (out-and-back) — Upper Falls View, Artist Point

The key thing: Artist Point (South Rim) and Lookout Point (North Rim) both look at the same Lower Falls — from opposite sides of the canyon. Artist Point is the classic postcard view; Lookout Point is the easy North-Rim view across the gorge. So the overlaps in the names are just different angles on the same two waterfalls.
⚠ Don't chase every pullout. Trying to hit every named overlook on both rims leads to fatigue, not better views. The two that matter are Artist Point (South Rim) and Brink of the Lower Falls (North Rim) — the core visual payoff is right there. If parking is chaotic, skip Grand View and Inspiration Point without regret.

Grand Teton
🧭 Where you are: Grand Teton's central and south zone — the tall-peaks half of the park, plus the town of Jackson. Reached over Teton Pass, about 45 min from Driggs.
The park's geography in one minute — and the "scenic drive" everyone mentions
Grand Teton is simple to picture: a wall of jagged peaks on the west, a flat sagebrush valley (Jackson Hole) on the east, and the Snake River winding between them. There are no foothills — the mountains erupt straight out of the valley floor, which is why they look so dramatic.

Two roads run north–south through that valley, and together they form the 42-Mile Scenic Loop — the drive people mean when they say "the Grand Teton scenic drive":

• Teton Park Road (the "inner road") — hugs the base of the peaks. Better views, and where Jenny Lake, String Lake, and Signal Mountain sit.
• US-89/191 (the "outer road") — runs up the east side of the valley. This is where the famous distant viewpoints are: Snake River Overlook, Mormon Row, Schwabacher Landing.

Today we drive both halves — north up the outer road for the classic viewpoints, then south down the inner road for the lakes and peaks. So we're doing the scenic loop without calling it that.

Jenny Lake ferry — have a plan B before you queue
In late July the line just to board the ferry can exceed 45 minutes by 10:00 AM. That's 45 minutes of standing still with kids, and it eats the buffer this day depends on.

The call to make on arrival (~9:35am): walk up, look at the ferry line, and decide immediately.

If the line is long → walk instead. The trail around the south end of the lake to Hidden Falls is 2.0 miles, flat and scenic. With a group this size, walking is often faster than standing in the queue — and far less frustrating. Nobody's stuck waiting.

Then take the ferry back. The return line moves much faster, so you still get the boat ride across the lake — just in the other direction, with no queue.

Either way the ~3 hrs holds. The point is to not default to the boat and lose an hour to a line.
🌅 Optional dawn start — for the photographers
  • ○Leaving Driggs at ~5:15am puts you in the valley for sunrise (~6am in July). Schwabacher Landing is the premier dawn spot — a quiet Snake River side channel where beaver dams still the water into a mirror, reflecting the whole range. Moose are common here at first light. Mormon Row also glows at sunrise. Only worth it for those who genuinely want it — no need to drag the whole group
🥾 Optional extension at Jenny Lake
  • ○Cascade Canyon — continue past Inspiration Point and the trail flattens into a wide glacial canyon between the peaks. Widely considered the best hike in the park, and the best moose odds on foot. Go as far as you like and turn back — even a mile in is worth it. Adds 1–2 hrs; the ferry back runs until early evening
  • ○Taggart Lake — a moderate 3–4 mi loop through wildflower meadows to a lake at the base of the peaks. Good alternative for anyone who'd rather hike than swim while the others are at String Lake
🫎 Moose-Wilson Road — optional, and we're not planning on it
  • ○The draw is real: an 8-mile road from Moose to Wilson through dense wetlands and hawthorn thickets — the most famous moose-and-bear road in Grand Teton.

    The reality: it's the narrowest paved road in the park, and roughly 1.5 miles of it is unpaved, dusty and rutted. The moment one moose steps near the road, traffic stops dead in both directions. With almost no shoulders or pullouts, a 20-minute drive becomes a 90-minute bumper-to-bumper crawl with no way out.

    The multi-car problem — this is the real reason: with five cars, keeping the group together here is impossible. If the lead car stops for a moose, cars three, four and five are stuck around a blind curve a quarter-mile back with no cell service to ask what's happening. That's a recipe for frustration at the end of a long day.

    What we do instead: at 4pm, hungry and tired, take US-89/191 — wide, fast, 55 mph, clear sightlines. It funnels all five cars straight into Jackson for dinner with no unpredictable delays.

    If a sub-group really wants the moose hunt and is happy to go it alone and meet the others in Jackson, this is where they'd do it.
